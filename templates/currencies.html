Why gold, not the dollar

Africa holds 40% of the world's gold reserves. Why price its currencies against someone else's paper?

The US Dollar became the world's reserve currency by political agreement in 1944 — not because it represented real wealth. African economies have been priced against a currency that has no physical backing, managed by institutions in which Africa has no meaningful vote.

Gold is different. It exists in the ground — much of it African ground. The DRC alone holds an estimated $24 trillion in mineral reserves. Ghana, South Africa, Mali, Burkina Faso, and Sudan are among the world's largest gold producers. When the Ghanaian Cedi is priced against gold rather than the dollar, it is priced against something Ghana actually has.

SOHANA's gold-anchored exchange is not just a currency tool. It is a philosophical and practical argument that intra-African trade should be denominated in Africa's own wealth.
